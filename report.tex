\section{Architecture and Scaling Justification}
% =====================================================================

We train a decoder-only transformer based on the Nemotron Nano-V3 architecture.
Our final submitted model is a \textbf{Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)} variant (experiment \texttt{full\_raw\_moe}) with 1.4B total parameters and 390M active per token. Table~\ref{tab:model_config} summarizes the configuration.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Final model configuration (\texttt{full\_raw\_moe}).}
\label{tab:model_config}
\begin{tabular}{ll|ll}
\toprule
\textbf{Param} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Param} & \textbf{Value} \\
\midrule
Total params      & 1,409M   & Experts / active   & 8 / top-2 \\
Active params     & 390M     & MoE aux loss       & 0.01 \\
Hidden ($d$)      & 768      & Context length     & 2,048 \\
Layers            & 24       & Vocab size         & 16,384 \\
Attn heads / KV   & 12 / 4   & Tied embeddings    & Yes \\
FFN per expert    & 3,072    & RoPE $\theta$      & 10,000 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The model uses Grouped-Query Attention (GQA, 3:1 ratio), SwiGLU MLPs, RoPE, and RMSNorm pre-normalization. Each MoE layer replaces the dense FFN with 8 expert MLPs; a learned gating network selects the top-2 per token. A load-balancing auxiliary loss ($\alpha{=}0.01$) encourages uniform expert utilization. Input and output embeddings are tied.

\paragraph{Scaling strategy.}
Training occurs on a single node with 8$\times$H100 80\,GB GPUs.
The MoE architecture scales total capacity to 1.4B parameters while keeping per-token compute equivalent to a 390M dense model.
Our best model trains on ${\approx}$55B tokens over one epoch, yielding ${\approx}$141$\times$ tokens per active parameter---well beyond Chinchilla-optimal 20$\times$, appropriate given MoE's parameter efficiency.
During early exploration we trained dense models at 4M, 39M, and 58M parameters following Chinchilla ratios (${\approx}$20$\times$) to validate the pipeline before scaling up (see Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}).

\section{Tokenizer}
% =====================================================================

We use a byte-level BPE tokenizer ($|\mathcal{V}|{=}16{,}384$) trained on the hybrid corpus with the HuggingFace \texttt{tokenizers} library.
Special tokens: \texttt{<|pad|>}, \texttt{<|eos|>}, \texttt{<|unk|>}; minimum frequency 2.
Training on the target corpus reduces fragmentation vs.\ a generic tokenizer.
The vocabulary size balances subword expressiveness with embedding cost ($16{,}384 \times 768 \approx 12.6$M tied parameters).

\subsection{Data Curation Pipeline}

We construct a multi-stage curation pipeline using NVIDIA NeMo Curator.

\paragraph{Unicode normalization and text cleaning.}
The first stage performs conservative mojibake repair (Latin-1 $\to$ UTF-8 re-decode when it reduces suspicious character counts), unescapes HTML entities, trims trailing reference/bibliography sections, and normalizes whitespace while preserving paragraph structure.

\paragraph{Heuristic filtering.}
Removes documents with malformed Unicode, extreme character distributions, or abnormal formatting patterns---eliminating truncated documents, boilerplate artifacts, and corrupted text.

\paragraph{Exact and fuzzy deduplication.}
Exact duplicates are removed by document hash. Near-duplicates are removed using MinHash LSH, eliminating templated pages and lightly modified copies.

\paragraph{Benchmark decontamination.}
Documents overlapping with evaluation benchmarks are filtered via $n$-gram matching to prevent train--test leakage.

\paragraph{Quality filtering.}
Remaining documents are scored using an LLM-based quality classifier (fineweb-edu-classifier). Documents scoring below 3.0 are removed, producing the final curated dataset.

\subsection{Synthetic Data Generation}

To improve coverage and diversify training signals, we generate
${\approx}$2M synthetic examples from ${\approx}$500k curated seed documents using a Data Designer pipeline backed by a locally-served
\textbf{NVIDIA Nemotron-3-Nano-30B-A3B} (MoE, 3.2B active parameters) model running on 4 vLLM replicas (each TP=2) behind a HAProxy load balancer.
Four prompt families are used:
\begin{itemize}
\item Open rewrites of existing content
\item Pedagogical teaching-style explanations
\item Question--answer pairs
\item Atomic factual statements
\end{itemize}
These transformations increase linguistic diversity and encourage reasoning behavior.
The final hybrid corpus combines curated text with ${\approx}$20\% synthetic content.
See Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic} for representative examples.

\section{Training}
\label{sec:training}
% =====================================================================

We conduct three groups of experiments on 8$\times$H100 80\,GB GPUs, isolating model capacity, data quality, and architecture effects.

\subsection{Training Infrastructure}

Models are distributed across 8 GPUs using Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP2). We apply \texttt{torch.compile} for graph-level kernel fusion. Training uses bfloat16 mixed precision throughout.

\paragraph{Optimizer.}
AdamW with $\beta_1{=}0.9$, $\beta_2{=}0.95$, $\epsilon{=}10^{-8}$, and weight decay 0.1 (applied to all parameters except biases and layer norms). Gradients are clipped at a max norm of 1.0.

\paragraph{Learning rate schedule.}
Linear warmup over the first 1\% of training steps followed by cosine decay to $0.5\times$ the peak learning rate of $3{\times}10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Data loading.}
Large datasets (raw, hybrid) are stored as pre-sharded JSONL files and consumed via a streaming data loader that tokenizes on-the-fly. Smaller datasets (curated) are tokenized in memory at startup.

\subsection{Experiment Groups}

\paragraph{Group 1: Model scaling (4M--58M params).}
Seven dense models on raw data, varying capacity from 4M to 58M parameters and data source at the 58M scale. These runs establish a baseline scaling trend and confirm that our pipeline produces expected improvements with increased capacity (see Appendix~\ref{app:scaling} for full table and results).

\paragraph{Group 2: Data quality at equal tokens.}
Three 382M dense models trained on ${\approx}$5.7B tokens each (raw, curated, hybrid) to isolate data quality from quantity.

\paragraph{Group 3: Full-scale data $\times$ architecture.}
Six runs forming a $3{\times}2$ grid: three data sources $\times$ two architectures (dense 382M / MoE 390M active, 1.4B total), each training for one full epoch.
Table~\ref{tab:group3} summarizes the configurations.

\subsection{Hardware, Wall-Clock Time, and Stability}

All training was performed on a single 8$\times$H100 80\,GB node. The largest run (raw MoE, 210K steps) took ${\approx}$49 hours wall-clock; the full-scale dense runs took ${\approx}$18 hours each; the equal-token and curated/hybrid runs completed in 1.5--2 hours each. Total compute across all 18 experiments: ${\approx}$150 GPU-hours.

The raw MoE run experienced one NCCL collective timeout near step 200K due to transient inter-GPU communication failure. Training resumed from the most recent checkpoint (saved every 5K steps) with negligible impact on final loss. All other runs completed without issues.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}
% =====================================================================

We evaluate all models zero-shot using the EleutherAI LM Evaluation Harness, the same backend used by NeMo Evaluator.
A custom wrapper (\texttt{NemotronNanoEvalLM}) implements the \texttt{lm\_eval.api.model.LM} interface for our architecture.

\subsection{Benchmarks}

We select a suite of nine zero-shot tasks appropriate for base (non-chat) language models:

\paragraph{Accuracy benchmarks (higher is better).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HellaSwag}: Commonsense sentence completion (acc\_norm).
    \item \textbf{PIQA}: Physical intuition QA (acc).
    \item \textbf{ARC-Easy / ARC-Challenge}: Grade-school science questions (acc\_norm).
    \item \textbf{SciQ}: Multiple-choice science QA (acc\_norm).
    \item \textbf{BoolQ}: Yes/no reading comprehension (acc).
    \item \textbf{WinoGrande}: Coreference resolution (acc).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Perplexity benchmarks (lower is better).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{WikiText}: Word-level perplexity on WikiText-103.
    \item \textbf{LAMBADA}: Perplexity on the LAMBADA last-word prediction task.
\end{itemize}

All evaluations use zero-shot prompting with no task-specific fine-tuning.
Raw evaluation outputs are saved per-run in \texttt{runs/<experiment>/eval\_results.json}.

\subsection{Results}

\paragraph{Group 1: Model scaling.}
Scaling from 4M to 58M parameters yields consistent gains across all benchmarks, with particularly large improvements on SciQ ($+$25.8\,pp) and BoolQ ($+$5.0\,pp). At fixed 58M size, data source matters little except for SciQ where more data helps substantially. See Appendix~\ref{app:group1} for figures.

\paragraph{Group 2: Curation helps per-token.}
At equal token budgets (${\approx}$5.7B), hybrid data wins 5/7 accuracy benchmarks (Figure~\ref{fig:group2_acc}, Table~\ref{tab:group2_results}), with ARC-C $+$4.9\,pp and ARC-E $+$5.5\,pp over raw. Raw retains an edge on perplexity (closer distribution to test sets). This confirms that \emph{at a fixed token budget, data curation and augmentation meaningfully improve downstream task performance}.

\paragraph{Finding 1: Volume beats quality at scale.}
The raw-data models dominate the overall leaderboard despite using unfiltered data. With ${\approx}$55B training tokens (10$\times$ the curated/hybrid corpora), the raw MoE achieves the best score on every benchmark. HellaSwag reaches 49.1\% (vs.\ 38.5\% for hybrid MoE), and WikiText perplexity drops to 17.6 (vs.\ 28.6). At current corpus sizes, we do not yet have enough curated data for quality to overcome the sheer advantage of quantity.

\paragraph{Finding 2: Curation helps per-token.}
Comparing curated and hybrid against raw \emph{at similar token counts} (5--9B), hybrid data consistently outperforms raw: hybrid MoE beats curated MoE on 6/7 accuracy benchmarks and achieves 7.3 points lower WikiText perplexity than the corresponding dense model.

\paragraph{Finding 3: MoE $>$ Dense for free.}
Across all three data sources, MoE improves over the matched dense model on nearly every metric, despite using the same active parameters per token. The gains are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item HellaSwag: $+$3.0 to $+$6.1\,pp
    \item PIQA: $+$2.2 to $+$2.4\,pp
    \item ARC-Easy: $+$2.3 to $+$4.5\,pp
    \item WikiText PPL: $-$3.3 to $-$7.3
\end{itemize}
The MoE advantage is largest on the raw corpus (where more data allows experts to specialize) and smallest on curated.

\paragraph{Summary.}
Our experiments reveal a clear hierarchy: (1)~data volume is the strongest lever at current scales, (2)~data curation improves sample efficiency when token budgets are matched, and (3)~sparse MoE routing provides a consistent ``free lunch'' over dense models at equivalent per-token compute. The best overall model---raw-data MoE with 1.4B total / 390M active parameters---achieves 49.1\% HellaSwag, 70.2\% PIQA, and 17.6 WikiText perplexity.

\section{Part A: NeMo Curator Lab}
\label{app:curator}

\paragraph{Objective}
The NeMo Curator lab explored downloading, processing, and curating a domain-specific slice of English Wikipedia using the NVIDIA NeMo Curator framework. The goal was to build a clean, deduplicated corpus for a chosen domain and evaluate whether curation measurably improves text quality via perplexity.

\paragraph{Infrastructure}
The lab ran on an 8$\times$H100 80GB node. NeMo Curator v1.1.0rc0 was used with a Ray cluster initialized at 32 CPUs and GPU-accelerated processing via \texttt{cuDF} 25.10.

\paragraph{Data acquisition and domain filtering}
Wikipedia data was downloaded from the February 2026 English dump (\texttt{enwiki-20260201}) using the \texttt{WikipediaDownloadExtractStage} pipeline. A custom \texttt{CategoriesAdder} stage extracted and appended per-document category lists by splitting on the final double-newline of each article. A \texttt{DomainFilter} then retained only documents whose categories matched any of the following keywords: \textit{computer science, computing, software, programming, algorithm, artificial intelligence, machine learning}. This produced \textbf{7 domain-relevant documents} from the downloaded sample.

\paragraph{Curation pipeline}
The curation pipeline (\texttt{wiki\_curation\_pipeline}) applied three sequential stages before deduplication:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{UnicodeReformatter} --- fixed mojibake and encoding artifacts using \texttt{ftfy}.
  \item \textbf{NewlineNormalizer} --- collapsed 3+ consecutive newlines to 2, standardizing paragraph structure.
  \item \textbf{WordCountFilter} (50--10,000 words) --- removed stubs and oversized disambiguation pages.
\end{itemize}
An \texttt{ExactDeduplicationWorkflow} then hashed each document's text to remove identical copies. No duplicates were found in this small sample, so the final curated dataset contained \textbf{6 documents} (one was dropped by the word-count filter).

\paragraph{Perplexity evaluation}
A \texttt{PerplexityScorer} Curator stage loaded GPT-2 on a GPU worker and computed per-document cross-entropy perplexity. Figure~\ref{fig:curator_ppl} compares distributions before and after curation.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{nb_nemo-c_cell44_img0.png}
\caption{GPT-2 perplexity distribution (left) and box plot (right) for non-curated ($n{=}7$, mean 39.4) vs.\ curated ($n{=}6$, mean 36.8) domain documents. Curation modestly reduces mean perplexity and tightens the distribution.}
\label{fig:curator_ppl}
\end{figure}

The curated corpus achieved a mean GPT-2 perplexity of \textbf{36.8} vs.\ \textbf{39.4} for the raw domain subset --- a modest improvement consistent with removing one outlier stub-like document via the word-count filter.

\section{Part A: NeMo Data Designer Lab}
\label{app:designer}

\paragraph{Objective}
The Data Designer lab built a synthetic pre-training data pipeline using NVIDIA NeMo Data Designer, backed by a locally-served \textbf{Nemotron-3-Nano-30B-A3B} (MoE, 3.2B active parameters) inference server. The goal was to augment the curated Wikipedia corpus with diverse rephrased variants to increase training signal per seed document.

\paragraph{Inference server and MFU}
The model was served via vLLM with 4 replicas (TP=2 each) behind a HAProxy load balancer. A throughput benchmark measured \textbf{343.3 output tokens/second}. The effective Model FLOP Utilization (MFU) was:
\[
\text{MFU} = \frac{\text{observed TFLOPS}}{\text{peak TFLOPS}} = \frac{2.20}{3956} \approx 0.06\%
\]
This extremely low MFU is expected during decode: with batch size $B \ll 295$ (the critical batch for compute-bound operation on H100), the kernel is entirely memory-bandwidth-bound, spending most cycles waiting on HBM reads rather than doing arithmetic.

\paragraph{Seed data}
The 18-document output from the Curator lab (\texttt{wiki\_curated\_domain\_data/}) was used as the seed corpus. The corpus spanned \textbf{144,675 characters} ($\approx$36,169 tokens) across topics including computing terminology, telecommunications engineering, and interoperability, with a mean document length of 8,038 characters.

\paragraph{Single-strategy rephrasing}
A Wikipedia-style open-rewrite strategy (\texttt{wiki\_rephrase}) prompted the model to produce clean, self-contained reference articles from each seed document. Evaluated on 3 documents (Figure~\ref{fig:designer_quality}), rephrasing reduced mean GPT-2 perplexity from \textbf{41.2 to 25.7}, indicating substantially more fluent and predictable prose. An LLM-as-judge evaluation (Nemotron-3-Nano-30B as the judge) scored the rephrased output at \textbf{4.7/5 coherence}, \textbf{5.0/5 faithfulness}, and \textbf{4.7/5 information density}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figA1_designer_quality.png}
\caption{Left: per-document GPT-2 perplexity before and after single-strategy wiki-style rephrasing (mean 41.2 $\to$ 25.7). Right: LLM-as-judge quality scores for coherence, faithfulness, and information density (all near 5/5).}
\label{fig:designer_quality}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Diverse QA generation}
A structured QA strategy generated exactly 3 grounded question--answer pairs per document using a Pydantic schema enforcing three question types: \texttt{yes\_no}, \texttt{open\_ended}, and \texttt{multiple\_choice}. The structured output was rendered into plain training text. This format increases factual density and encourages the model to learn explicit question-answering behavior from pre-training data.

\paragraph{Multi-strategy pipeline}
A four-strategy pipeline was assembled to maximize linguistic diversity per seed document, following the BeyondWeb approach of applying multiple independent rephrasing transforms:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{distill} --- concise summary retaining only the core concepts ($\approx$602 output tokens/doc).
  \item \textbf{knowledge\_extract} --- verbatim-style extraction of key facts and definitions ($\approx$2,009 tokens/doc).
  \item \textbf{knowledge\_list} --- numbered lists of atomic factual statements ($\approx$1,004 tokens/doc).
  \item \textbf{diverse\_qa} --- structured QA pairs as above ($\approx$803 tokens/doc).
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:multistrategy} shows the per-strategy compute cost. The total pipeline consumed $1.43 \times 10^{15}$ FLOPs across all 18 seed documents --- approximately \textbf{3.1$\times$} the cost of a single-strategy run --- with an estimated wall-clock time under 0.5~ms on 4$\times$H100 at observed MFU.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figA2_multistrategy_cost.png}
\caption{Per-strategy FLOPs for the 4-strategy multi-strategy pipeline. \texttt{knowledge\_extract} dominates due to its longer output length ($\approx$2,009 tokens/doc vs.\ $\approx$600--800 for the other strategies).}
\label{fig:multistrategy}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Takeaway}
The Data Designer lab demonstrated that a locally-served MoE model can produce high-quality, diverse synthetic training documents at minimal additional compute cost relative to dense alternatives. The multi-strategy approach converts each seed document into 4 distinct training examples, effectively quadrupling the usable training corpus while preserving factual grounding (faithfulness 5.0/5) and improving linguistic fluency (perplexity 41.2 $\to$ 25.7).
